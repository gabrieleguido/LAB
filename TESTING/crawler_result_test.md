Bermuda Triangle - Wikipedia
The **Bermuda Triangle** , also known as the **Devil's Triangle** , is a loosely defined region in the [North Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean"), roughly bounded by [Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida "Florida"), [Bermuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda "Bermuda"), and [Puerto Rico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico "Puerto Rico"). Since the mid-20th century, it has been the focus of an [urban legend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_legend "Urban legend") suggesting that many [aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aircraft "Aircraft"), [ships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship "Ship"), and people have disappeared there under mysterious circumstances. However, extensive investigations by reputable sources, including the [U.S. government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._government "U.S. government") and scientific organizations, have found no evidence of unusual activity, attributing reported incidents to natural phenomena, human error, and misinterpretation.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Kusche,_1975-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-NOVA,1976-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-WoodsHole_Bermuda-3)
Although the nearby [Sargasso Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sargasso_Sea "Sargasso Sea") already had a reputation as a mysterious region where ships may become lost,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-heller-4) the earliest suggestion of unusual disappearances in the Bermuda area appeared in an article written by Edward Van Winkle Jones of the _[Miami Herald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miami_Herald "Miami Herald")_ that was distributed by the [Associated Press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_Press "Associated Press") and appeared in various American newspapers on 17 September 1950.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Jones,1950-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-7)
Two years later, _[Fate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fate_\(magazine\) "Fate \(magazine\)")_ magazine published "Sea Mystery at Our Back Door": a short article, by George X. Sand, that was the first to lay out the now-familiar triangular area where the losses took place.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Sand-8) Sand recounted the loss of several planes and ships since World War II: the disappearance of _Sandra_ , a [tramp steamer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tramp_steamer "Tramp steamer");[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-10) the December 1945 loss of [Flight 19](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_19 "Flight 19"), a group of five US Navy [torpedo bombers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torpedo_bomber "Torpedo bomber") on a training mission; the January 1948 disappearance of _[Star Tiger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BSAA_Star_Tiger_disappearance "BSAA Star Tiger disappearance")_ , a [British South American Airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_South_American_Airways "British South American Airways") (BSAA) passenger airplane; the March 1948 disappearance of a fishing [skiff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skiff "Skiff") with three men, including jockey [Albert Snider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Snider "Albert Snider");[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-12) the December 1948 disappearance of an [Airborne Transport DC-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Airborne_Transport_DC-3_disappearance "1948 Airborne Transport DC-3 disappearance") charter flight en route from Puerto Rico to Miami; and the January 1949 disappearance of _[Star Ariel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BSAA_Star_Ariel_disappearance "BSAA Star Ariel disappearance")_ ,[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-13) another BSAA passenger airplane.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Sand-8)
Flight 19 was covered again in the April 1962 issue of _The American Legion Magazine_.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Eckert,1962-14)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-AmLegion-15) In it, author [Allan W. Eckert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allan_W._Eckert "Allan W. Eckert") wrote that the flight leader had been heard saying, "We cannot be sure of any direction ... everything is wrong ... strange ... the ocean doesn't look as it should."[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-AmLegion-15) In February 1964, [Vincent Gaddis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincent_Gaddis "Vincent Gaddis") wrote an article called "The Deadly Bermuda Triangle" in _[Argosy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argosy_\(magazine\) "Argosy \(magazine\)")_ saying Flight 19 and other disappearances were part of a pattern of strange events in the region, dating back to at least 1840.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Gaddis,1964-16)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-17) The next year, Gaddis expanded this article into a book, _Invisible Horizons_.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Gaddis,1965-18)
Other writers elaborated on Gaddis' ideas, including John Wallace Spencer (_Limbo of the Lost_ , 1969, repr. 1973);[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-ReferenceA-19) [Charles Berlitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Berlitz "Charles Berlitz") (_[The Bermuda Triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bermuda_Triangle_\(book\) "The Bermuda Triangle \(book\)")_ , 1974);[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-ReferenceB-20) and [Richard Winer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Winer "Richard Winer") (_The Devil's Triangle_ , 1974).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Winer,_1974-21) Various of these authors incorporated supernatural elements.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-22)
Sand's article in _Fate_ described the area as "a watery triangle bounded roughly by [Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida "Florida"), [Bermuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda "Bermuda") and [Puerto Rico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico "Puerto Rico")".[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Sand-8): 12  The _Argosy_ article by Gaddis further delineated the boundaries,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Gaddis,1964-16) giving its [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_\(geometry\) "Vertex \(geometry\)") as [Miami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miami "Miami"), [San Juan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico "San Juan, Puerto Rico"), and Bermuda. Subsequent writers did not necessarily follow this definition.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-navy,1996-23) Some writers gave different boundaries and vertices to the triangle, with the total area varying from 1.3 to 3.9 million km2 (0.50 to 1.51 million sq mi),[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-navy,1996-23) in some cases expanding the boundary of the triangle as far as the coast of Ireland.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-NOVA,1976-2) Consequently, the determination of which accidents occurred inside the triangle depends on which writer reported them.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-navy,1996-23)
[Larry Kusche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larry_Kusche "Larry Kusche"), author of _The Bermuda Triangle Mystery: Solved_ (1975),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Kusche,_1975-1) argued that many claims of Gaddis and subsequent writers were exaggerated, dubious or unverifiable. Kusche's research revealed a number of inaccuracies and inconsistencies between Berlitz's accounts and statements from eyewitnesses, participants, and others involved in the initial incidents. Kusche noted cases where pertinent information went unreported, such as the disappearance of round-the-world yachtsman [Donald Crowhurst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Crowhurst "Donald Crowhurst"), which Berlitz had presented as a mystery, despite clear evidence to the contrary. Another example was the ore-carrier recounted by Berlitz as lost without trace three days out of an Atlantic port when in fact it had been lost three days out of a port with the same name in the _Pacific_ Ocean. Kusche also argued that a large percentage of the incidents that sparked allegations of the Triangle's mysterious influence actually occurred well outside it. Often his research was simple: he would review period newspapers of the dates of reported incidents and find reports on possibly relevant events, like unusual weather, that were never mentioned in the disappearance stories.

When the British [Channel 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Channel_4 "Channel 4") television program _The Bermuda Triangle_ (1992)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-BFI-25) was being produced by John Simmons of Geofilms for the _Equinox_ series, the marine insurance market [Lloyd's of London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lloyd%27s_of_London "Lloyd's of London") was asked if an unusually large number of ships had sunk in the Bermuda Triangle area. Lloyd's determined that large numbers of ships had not sunk there.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-WoodsHole_Bermuda-3) Lloyd's does not charge higher rates for passing through this area. [United States Coast Guard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard "United States Coast Guard") records confirm their conclusion. In fact, the number of supposed disappearances is relatively insignificant considering the number of ships and aircraft that pass through on a regular basis.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Kusche,_1975-1)
The Coast Guard is also officially skeptical of the Triangle, noting that they collect and publish, through their inquiries, much documentation contradicting many of the incidents written about by the Triangle authors. In one such incident involving the 1972 explosion and sinking of the tanker [_V. A. Fogg_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SS_V._A._Fogg "SS V. A. Fogg"), the Coast Guard photographed the wreck and recovered several bodies,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-USCG_Fogg-26) in contrast with one Triangle author's claim that all the bodies had vanished, with the exception of the captain, who was found sitting in his cabin at his desk, clutching a coffee cup.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-ReferenceA-19) In addition, _V. A. Fogg_ sank off the coast of [Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas "Texas"), nowhere near the commonly accepted boundaries of the Triangle. 
_[Nova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova_\(American_TV_program\) "Nova \(American TV program\)")_ /_[Horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon_\(British_TV_series\) "Horizon \(British TV series\)")_ aired the episode "The Case of the Bermuda Triangle" on 27 June 1976. The episode was highly critical, stating that "When we've gone back to the original sources or the people involved, the mystery evaporates. Science does not have to answer questions about the Triangle because those questions are not valid in the first place [...] Ships and planes behave in the Triangle the same way they behave everywhere else in the world."[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-NOVA,1976-2)
[Skeptical researchers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_skepticism "Scientific skepticism"), such as Ernest Taves[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Taves,1978-27) and Barry Singer,[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Singer,1979-28) have noted how mysteries and the paranormal are very popular and profitable. This has led to the production of vast amounts of material on topics such as the Bermuda Triangle. They were able to show that some of the pro-paranormal material is often misleading or inaccurate, but its producers continue to market it. Accordingly, they have claimed that the market is biased in favor of books, TV specials, and other media that support the Triangle mystery, and against well-researched material if it espouses a skeptical viewpoint. 
In a 2013 study, the [World Wide Fund for Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature "World Wide Fund for Nature") identified the world's 10 most dangerous waters for shipping, but the Bermuda Triangle was not among them.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-29)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-30)
[Benjamin Radford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Radford "Benjamin Radford"), an author and scientific paranormal investigator, noted in an interview on the Bermuda Triangle that it could be very difficult to locate an aircraft lost at sea due to the vast search area, and although the disappearance might be mysterious, that did not make it paranormal or unexplainable. Radford further noted the importance of double-checking information as the mystery surrounding the Bermuda Triangle had been created by people who had neglected to do so.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-31)
[NOAA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration "National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration") attributes most Bermuda Triangle disappearances to environmental factors such as hurricanes, sudden weather shifts from the Gulf Stream, and hazardous shallow waters. The U.S. Navy dismisses supernatural claims, emphasizing natural causes and human error. Additionally, the [U.S. Board on Geographic Names](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Board_on_Geographic_Names "United States Board on Geographic Names") does not list the Bermuda Triangle as an official location, given the lack of evidence distinguishing it from other ocean regions.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-32)
In the 2010s and 2020s, scientists and researchers have reiterated that the Bermuda Triangle does not exhibit higher rates of disappearances than other heavily traveled ocean regions. Australian scientist [Karl Kruszelnicki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Kruszelnicki "Karl Kruszelnicki") told _The Independent_ in 2017 that the "mystery" can be explained by human error, unpredictable weather, and the sheer volume of traffic passing through the area. According to Kruszelnicki, "On a percentage basis, the number of accidents in the Bermuda Triangle is the same as anywhere else in the world."[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-33)
Those accepting the Bermuda Triangle as a real phenomenon have suggested a number of explanations. 
Triangle writers have used a number of supernatural concepts to explain the events. One explanation pins the blame on leftover technology from the mythical lost city or state of [Atlantis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantis "Atlantis"). Sometimes connected to the Atlantis story is the submerged rock formation known as the [Bimini Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bimini_Road "Bimini Road") off the island of [Bimini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bimini "Bimini") in the Bahamas, which is in the Triangle by some definitions. Followers of the purported psychic [Edgar Cayce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edgar_Cayce "Edgar Cayce") take his prediction that evidence of Atlantis would be found in 1968 as referring to the discovery of the Bimini Road. Believers describe the formation as a road, wall, or other structure, but the Bimini Road is of natural origin.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-CSI,_2004-34)
Some hypothesize that a parallel universe exists in the Bermuda Triangle region, causing a time/space warp that sucks the objects around it into a parallel universe.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-35) Others attribute the events to [UFOs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UFO "UFO").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-36)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Cochran,_2003-37) [Charles Berlitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Berlitz "Charles Berlitz"), author of various books on anomalous phenomena, lists several theories attributing the losses in the Triangle to anomalous or unexplained forces.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-ReferenceB-20)
[Compass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass "Compass") issues are frequently cited in accounts of Triangle incidents. While some have theorized that unusual local magnetic anomalies may exist in the area,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-USNAVY-38) such anomalies have not been found. Compasses have natural [magnetic variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_variation "Magnetic variation") in relation to the [magnetic poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Magnetic_poles "Poles of astronomical bodies"), a fact navigators have known for centuries. Only for a small number of places are [magnetic (compass) north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_north_pole "Magnetic north pole") and [geographic (true) north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_north "True north") exactly the same‍— in the United States, for example, as of 2000[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bermuda_Triangle&action=edit), magnetic north and true north will appear to be the same only in those places located along a line running from [Wisconsin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wisconsin "Wisconsin") to the [Gulf of Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico "Gulf of Mexico").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-39) Members of the public who are unaware of this, however, may think there is something mysterious about a compass "changing" across an area as large as the Triangle, which it naturally will.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Kusche,_1975-1)
The [Gulf Stream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Stream "Gulf Stream") ([Florida Current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida_Current "Florida Current")) is a major surface current, primarily driven by [thermohaline circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation "Thermohaline circulation") that originates in the Gulf of Mexico and then flows through the [Straits of Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straits_of_Florida "Straits of Florida") into the North Atlantic. In essence, it is a river within an ocean, and, like a river, it can and does carry floating objects.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Winer,_1974,_pp._145%E2%80%93146-40) It has a maximum surface velocity of about 2 m/s (6.6 ft/s).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-41) A small plane making a [water landing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_landing "Water landing") or a boat having engine trouble can be carried away from its reported position by the current.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Berlitz,_p._77-42)
One of the most cited explanations in official inquiries as to the loss of any aircraft or vessel is [human error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_error "Human error").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-43) Human stubbornness may have caused businessman [Harvey Conover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Conover "Harry Conover") to lose his sailing yacht, _Revonoc_ , as he sailed into the teeth of a storm south of Florida on 1 January 1958.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-44)
Hurricanes are powerful storms which form in tropical waters and have cost thousands of lives and caused billions of dollars in damage. The sinking of [Francisco de Bobadilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francisco_de_Bobadilla "Francisco de Bobadilla")'s Spanish fleet in 1502 was the first recorded instance of a destructive hurricane.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Winer,_1974,_pp._25%E2%80%9328-45) These storms have caused a number of incidents related to the Triangle. Many Atlantic hurricanes pass through the Triangle as they recurve off the Eastern Seaboard, and, before the advent of [weather satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_satellite "Weather satellite"), ships often had little to no warning of a hurricane's approach.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-46)
A powerful [downdraft of cold air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microburst "Microburst") was suspected to be a cause in the sinking of _[Pride of Baltimore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pride_of_Baltimore "Pride of Baltimore")_ on 14 May 1986. The crew of the sunken vessel noted the wind suddenly shifted and increased velocity from 32 km/h (20 mph) to 97–145 km/h (60–90 mph). A National Hurricane Center satellite specialist, James Lushine, stated "during very unstable weather conditions the downburst of cold air from aloft can hit the surface like a bomb, exploding outward like a giant squall line of wind and water."[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-47)
An explanation for some of the disappearances has focused on the presence of large fields of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") hydrates (a form of natural gas) on the [continental shelves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_shelves "Continental shelves").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Energy_Citations_Database_\(ECD\)_%E2%80%93_Document_#616279-48) Laboratory experiments carried out in Australia have proven that bubbles can, indeed, sink a scale model ship by decreasing the density of the water,[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Monash,_2003-49)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-50)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-51) and any wreckage would be deposited on the ocean floor or rapidly dispersed by the Gulf Stream. It has been hypothesized that periodic methane [eruptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eruption "Eruption") (sometimes called "[mud volcanoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mud_volcano "Mud volcano")") may produce regions of frothy water that are no longer capable of providing adequate [buoyancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buoyancy "Buoyancy") for ships. If this were the case, such an area forming around a ship could cause it to sink very rapidly and without warning.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Joseph,-52)
Publications by the [USGS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USGS "USGS") describe large stores of undersea hydrates worldwide, including the [Blake Ridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blake_Ridge "Blake Ridge") area, off the coast of the southeastern United States.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-WoodsHole_papers-53) However, according to the USGS, no large releases of gas hydrates are believed to have occurred in the Bermuda Triangle for the past 15,000 years.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-WoodsHole_Bermuda-3)
The sail training ship HMS _Atalanta_ (originally named HMS _Juno_) disappeared with her entire crew after setting sail from the [Royal Naval Dockyard, Bermuda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Naval_Dockyard,_Bermuda "Royal Naval Dockyard, Bermuda") for [Falmouth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall "Falmouth, Cornwall"), [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England "England") on 31 January 1880.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-54) It was presumed that she sank in a powerful [storm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storm "Storm") which crossed her route a couple of weeks after she sailed, and that her crew being composed primarily of inexperienced trainees may have been a contributing factor. The search for evidence of her fate attracted worldwide attention at the time (connection is also often made to the 1878 loss of the training ship [HMS _Eurydice_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HMS_Eurydice_\(1843\) "HMS Eurydice \(1843\)"), which foundered after departing the Royal Naval Dockyard in Bermuda for Portsmouth on 6 March), and she was alleged decades later to have been a victim of the mysterious triangle, an allegation resoundingly refuted by the research of author [David Francis Raine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Francis_Raine "David Francis Raine") in 1997.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-55)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-56)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-57)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-58)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-59)
The incident resulting in the single largest loss of life in the history of the US Navy not related to combat occurred when the collier _Cyclops_ , carrying a full load of [manganese ore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manganese_ore "Manganese ore") and with one engine out of action, went missing without a trace with a crew of 306 sometime after 4 March 1918, after departing the island of [Barbados](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbados "Barbados"). Although there is no strong evidence for any single theory, many independent theories exist, some blaming storms, some capsizing, and some suggesting that [wartime enemy activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") was to blame for the loss.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-DMerrill-60)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Bermuda_Cruises-61) In addition, two of _Cyclops_ 's sister ships, [_Proteus_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USS_Proteus_\(AC-9\) "USS Proteus \(AC-9\)") and [_Nereus_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USS_Nereus_\(AC-10\) "USS Nereus \(AC-10\)"), were subsequently lost in the North Atlantic during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"). Both ships were transporting heavy loads of metallic ore similar to that which was loaded on _Cyclops_ during her fatal voyage.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Kusche,_pp._93%E2%80%9394-62) In all three cases structural failure due to overloading with a much denser cargo than designed is considered the most likely cause of sinking. 
_Carroll A. Deering_ , a five-masted [schooner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schooner "Schooner") built in 1919, was found hard aground and abandoned at [Diamond Shoals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond_Shoals "Diamond Shoals"), near [Cape Hatteras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Hatteras "Cape Hatteras"), [North Carolina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Carolina "North Carolina"), on 31 January 1921. [FBI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation "Federal Bureau of Investigation") investigation into the _Deering_ scrutinized, then ruled out, multiple theories as to why and how the ship was abandoned, including piracy, domestic Communist sabotage and the involvement of [rum-runners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rum-runner "Rum-runner").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-63)
Flight 19 was a training flight of five [TBM Avenger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grumman_TBF_Avenger "Grumman TBF Avenger") torpedo bombers that disappeared on 5 December 1945, while over the Atlantic. The squadron's flight plan was scheduled to take them due east from [Fort Lauderdale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale "Fort Lauderdale") for 141 mi (227 km), north for 73 mi (117 km), and then back over a final 140 mi (225 km) leg to complete the exercise. The flight never returned to base. The disappearance was attributed by Navy investigators to navigational error leading to the aircraft running out of fuel. 
One of the search and rescue aircraft deployed to look for them, a [PBM Mariner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_PBM_Mariner "Martin PBM Mariner") with a 13-man crew, also disappeared. A tanker off the coast of Florida reported seeing an explosion[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-The_Loss_of_Flight_19-64) and observing a widespread oil slick when fruitlessly searching for survivors. The weather was becoming stormy by the end of the incident.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-65) According to contemporaneous sources, the Mariner had a history of explosions due to vapor leaks when heavily loaded with fuel, as it might have been for a potentially long search-and-rescue operation.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Leadbeater-2020-66)
[G-AHNP _Star Tiger_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BSAA_Star_Tiger_disappearance "BSAA Star Tiger disappearance") disappeared on 30 January 1948, on a flight from the [Azores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azores "Azores") to Bermuda; [G-AGRE _Star Ariel_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BSAA_Star_Ariel_disappearance "BSAA Star Ariel disappearance") disappeared on 17 January 1949, on a flight from Bermuda to [Kingston, Jamaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica "Kingston, Jamaica"). Both were [Avro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avro "Avro") [Tudor IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tudor_IV "Tudor IV") passenger aircraft operated by [British South American Airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_South_American_Airways "British South American Airways").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-67) Both planes were operating at the very limits of their range and the slightest error or fault in the equipment could keep them from reaching the small island.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Kusche,_1975-1)
On 28 December 1948, a [Douglas DC-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_DC-3 "Douglas DC-3") aircraft, number [NC16002](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Airborne_Transport_DC-3_disappearance "1948 Airborne Transport DC-3 disappearance"), disappeared while on a flight from San Juan, Puerto Rico, to Miami. No trace of the aircraft, or the 32 people on board, was ever found. A Civil Aeronautics Board investigation found there was insufficient information available on which to determine probable cause of the disappearance.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Aviation_Safety_DC3-68)
A pleasure yacht was found adrift in the Atlantic south of Bermuda on 26 September 1955; it is usually stated in the stories (Berlitz, Winer)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-ReferenceB-20)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Winer,_1974-21) that the crew vanished while the yacht survived being at sea during three hurricanes. On the morning of September 22, _Connemara IV_ was lying to a heavy mooring in the open [roadstead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roadstead "Roadstead") of [Carlisle Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlisle_Bay,_Barbados "Carlisle Bay, Barbados"). Because of the approaching hurricane, the owner strengthened the mooring ropes and put out two additional anchors. There was little else he could do, as the exposed mooring was the only available anchorage. ... In Carlisle Bay, the sea in the wake of [Hurricane Janet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurricane_Janet "Hurricane Janet") was awe-inspiring and dangerous. The owner of _Connemara IV_ observed that she had disappeared. An investigation revealed that she had dragged her moorings and gone to sea.
On 28 August 1963, a pair of [US Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Air_Force "US Air Force") [KC-135 Stratotanker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KC-135_Stratotanker "KC-135 Stratotanker") aircraft collided and crashed into the Atlantic 300 miles (480 km) west of Bermuda.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-70)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-71) Some writers[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Gaddis,1964-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-ReferenceB-20)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Winer,_1974-21) say that while the two aircraft did collide, there were two distinct crash sites, separated by over 160 miles (260 km) of water. However, Kusche's research showed that the unclassified version of the Air Force investigation report revealed that the debris field defining the second "crash site" was examined by a search and rescue ship, and found to be a mass of [seaweed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seaweed "Seaweed") and [driftwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Driftwood "Driftwood") tangled in an old [buoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buoy "Buoy").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle#cite_note-Kusche,_1975-1)
